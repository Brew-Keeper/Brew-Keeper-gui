Good Coffee

Making coffee in a typical coffee maker can be as simple as scooping pre-ground coffee into a filter, pouring water into its reservoir, and turning it on. If you were looking to make better coffee, you might buy specialty beans and even freshly grind them. Even so, there is not much control over the flavor—those beans may not produce good-tasting coffee in your machine. The temperature of the water and the manner and duration in which it is applied to the grounds—three very important factors in determining final flavor—are predetermined in most coffee makers.

So, if you did have complete control over all aspects of the process, what method should you use to get the best-tasting coffee? As it turns out, the answer to this question is the start of a journey—everyone’s tongue has a slightly different idea of what “best” is, but also different beans need different treatment to accentuate their positive features (or minimize negative ones).

Coffee Controls

Ingredients: the beans (kind, grind, and amount) and water (kind, temperature, and amount) Brew Method: combining water and ground coffee in some fashion, then usually separating the liquid from the grounds

AeroPress

The AeroPress allows a brewer such control. At first, the range of possibilities for each aspect can be overwhelming, not to mention the possible combinations and remembering what produced good results and what didn’t. And starting with a bean you are unfamiliar with can be like starting all over again. If only there was a way to keep track of your coffee explorations . . .
